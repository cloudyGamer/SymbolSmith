  • Locations

Symbol Smith provides marks for are 3 Locations:

London Birmingham Dublin

London

Guild Mark: Leopard's head or Lion's Passant

Both vary in aspects, the leopards head was surmounted with a crown between 1478-1820. Sometimes on small pieces it was omitted altogether especially between 1790-1820.

London had a very high standard of design and workmanship over the centuries. The work showed a lot of individuality in conception and execution of pieces. They have recieved world admiration.



Birmingham

Guild Mark: Anchor accompanied by Lion Passant

Established by the Act of Parliament in 1773.

During the 20th Century an anchor placed on its side represented gold and an upright mark represented silver. From 1999 on it was placed on its side for all metals



Dublin

Guild Mark: Harp Crowned. From 1731 figure of Hibernia was added

Hallmarking in Ireland began in middle of 17th century. A whole range of silver craft was found bearing the Dublin mark. Dublin was known for its excellent workmanship and designs of a decorative nature. From the 18th century, the influence of emigrant goldsmiths can be seen. Dublin was unaffected by 1973 British hallmarking Act.

  • What are Hallmarks?

A hallmark is mark stamped on articles of gold, silver, or platinum by the British assay offices, certifying their standard of purity. Most articles of silver will bear marks these which are embedded by its manufacturer and enable the years and origin to be traced. Three key marks, the standard, the assay office & the date letter can determine its finness & value.

For centuries British silver is protected by the stamping of these symbols and letters. Thanks to the "date letter" any piece of British sterling silver can be exactly dated.

Hallmarks are applied with a hammer and punch, a process that leaves sharp edges and spurs of metal. Therefore, hallmarking is generally done before the piece goes for its final polishing. See More >>

No Marks??

If the piece has other marks it could be silver plated or silver from outside Britain. If it has no marks at all, The marks may have been rubbed off but it cannot be legally sold as silver. It is instead sold as white metal.

  • Hallmark Categories

Most items will contain marks from each of these categories, the first three are the most important. The other categories will help produce more accurate results

        

Region

    

Also know as Standard Marks, Show the Fineness or Purity of the silver. In England there have been two standards to which silver was assayed, Sterling & Britannia. From 1720, Sterling became the legally required standard.

     

Period

    

Also known as Assay Office or Town marks. They enable the location of the assay office that tested and hallmarked the article to be traced but it’s not necessarily where it was manufactured. Marks differed at each assay office.

        

Date Letter

    

Letters of the alphabet are used as marks to represent the date of manufacture. Only from 1975 did all date letters coincide with the calender year. The various cycles of date letters can be identified by their forms & shape of their shields.

 
        

Additional

    

These are not part of the marks that show fineness, but if absent when they should be present that should raise a question.

Duty Marks - A mark of the sovereigns head showed duty was paid. Variable tax was levied on all silver in Great Britain. However marks were slow to change of on death of the sovereigns, some offices took several years to change.

Other marks include the Draw-Back when duty was refunded 1784-85, the Kings Jubilee to commemorate 25th anniversary of his majesty, 1935-36 and the Cornation mark of Queen Elizabeth in 1953.

        

Makers

    

Although we don't provide access to makers marks, it is important to know they will also appear on most silver items. Makers Marks - Represent the craft persons responsible for design, manufacture, decorating, finishing and selling the article. From 1697-1720 by law the first two letters of your surname was used. From 1999 they became known as sponsor’s mark. There are large volumes of makers marks.
